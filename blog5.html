Real-World Examples of Steganography: Hiding Secrets Across the Ages

From shaved heads and invisible ink to digital covert channels, steganography's history is as fascinating as its future.

By The Covert Comm. Team | November 15, 2025

Steganography has a rich and long history, predating digital computers by millennia. The practice of **hiding the message itself** has always been a powerful tool in espionage, warfare, and covert communication. Examining these examples helps us understand why digital steganography remains relevant today.

Historical & Classical Examples 🏛️

Ancient Greece (c. 440 BC)
The Shaved Head Message

The Greek tyrant **Histiaeus** needed to send a secret message. He shaved a slave's head, tattooed the message on his scalp, and waited for the hair to grow back. The "cover message" was the slave himself, appearing unremarkable. Once delivered, the recipient simply shaved the slave's head again to read the secret.

World War I & II
The Citrus Juice Letter

A simple, yet effective method involved **invisible ink**. Agents would write seemingly innocent letters, but hide secret messages between the lines using substances like lemon juice, milk, or urine. The "stego-key" was the simple act of heating the paper to reveal the hidden text.

Modern & Digital Cyber Examples 💻

Today, the covert channel is often a digital file. Instead of hiding text on paper, data is hidden within the **redundancy** of images, audio, and video files.

Terrorism & Espionage
The Jihadi Image Files

Following the 9/11 attacks, reports suggested that Al-Qaeda operatives communicated by hiding messages or even entire training manuals within innocuous-looking **JPEG image files** posted to public forums. While widely reported, this demonstrated the accessibility and utility of digital steganography for covert communication.

Cybercrime (e.g., LokiBot)
Stealthy Command and Control (C&C)

Advanced persistent threats (APTs) and malware like **LokiBot** have used steganography to hide data. For instance, the malware might embed its C&C server address or even its next-stage code within a harmless **PNG file** downloaded from a legitimate website, helping it evade network firewalls and intrusion detection systems.

Applications in Digital Watermarking 🔐

The Legal Side of Steganography

Steganography is not always malicious. It is a core technology for **digital watermarking**, where a copyright message, serial number, or owner ID is permanently and invisibly embedded into commercial images or music to track unauthorized distribution. This provides proof of ownership without degrading the media quality.

Whether etched into a bald head or hidden in the LSB of a holiday photo, steganography is defined by one enduring principle: **the existence of the message is the ultimate secret.**
